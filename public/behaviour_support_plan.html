Primary Clinical Formulation
Understanding the "Why" behind Bronwyn's behaviours
1. Primary Behavioral Profiles & Core Drivers
⚡ The "Control vs. Chaos" Drive

Bronwyn's behaviours (including sudden hitting) are primarily driven by an acute need for predictability and external environmental ordering. When her strict internal rules are breached — e.g., Mom choosing a breakfast food out of turn, Dad returning food to the fridge half-eaten, or visible injuries on others — she experiences instantaneous neurological threat.

Unable to process this via complex expressive verbal language, she discharges this sudden internal panic physically through hitting.

🍽️ Food Throwing & Completion Dynamics

Half-eaten or visible "saved" food violates her rigid rule of "closed" items. This triggers an obsessive need to force consumption or completely discard the items to resolve her visual anxiety.

👤 Professional-PDA Boundary Dynamic
Parents = "Safe" Attachment

Parents represent "safe" attachments where she feels empowered to enforce her own ordering systems — bossing, dictating stereo input, and rejecting hygiene items.

Mr. Minton = "System Framework"

The support worker represents a formal "system framework." Her initial defensive comment ("Bye, Mr. Minton") and reference to "School" indicate she is hyper-aware that his presence challenges her established control.

Critical
Arousal & Crisis Response
Four escalation stages with phase-specific interventions
1. GREEN Phase — Baseline / Regulated
Objectives: Consolidate safety triggers & respect routine rules.
The Serviette Accommodation
Ensure white serviettes are always readily available and physically presented with foods. Do not force direct touching.
"Mom's Choice" Visuals
Proactively schedule breakfast options so variations are visual events rather than surprises.
2. YELLOW Phase — The "Rumble" Stage
Warning Signs: Vocal repetitions, pacing, phone wiping, ordering parents.
Immediate Non-Verbal Interventions
Introduce "change cards" without complex speaking. Switch to quiet, low tone.
Declarative Statements
Avoid telling her what to do. Use: "I can see the cereal box is in the bin. Let's step away."
3. RED Phase — Crisis / Hitting
Intervention: Maximum Distance, Total Silence, Block Safely.
Evacuate & Step Back
Both worker and parent must physically disengage. Speak 0 words.
No Eye Contact
Looking at her reinforces challenge loops. Look down or away while securing yourself behind a physical barrier (counter/sofa cushion).
4. BLUE Phase — Recovery / Fatigue
Intervention: Low Demands, Extended Rest.
The Recovery Window
Expect cortisol levels to remain elevated for up to 3 hours. Avoid asking her to choose anything or do any tasks.
Refusal Acceptance
Accept she may be highly fragile or lethargic; let her reset without commentary.

2. Tactical Core Toolbox
🍽️ Food Throwing & "Completion" Rules

She wants to control what is eaten. Half-eaten or "saved" food violates her rule of "closed" items, initiating an obsession to force consumption or discard.

Direct Protocol: Clean Counter Policy

All parental food prep and leftovers must be immediately closed and stored behind closed cupboard doors/fridges out of direct sight line.

Verbal Script: If she attempts to force her parent to finish food, use flat tone: "Dad's food is finished. It is away." Do not debate or justify. Walk away immediately.

🤍 Sensory Shields (White Serviette)

White napkins act as a mandatory protective shield against food textures. Her refusal of tissues from parents but acceptance from support workers is a direct test of relational boundaries.

Direct Protocol: Systemic Uniformity & Deputized Control

Systemic Uniformity: All staff and parents must use identical white serviettes. Stock them in identical plastic boxes.

Deputize Control: Before meals: "Can you please put three clean serviettes on the table?" This translates bossing into a helpful structuring task.

🩹 Injury & Hygiene Hyper-Fixations

Skin disruptions cause high tactile panic. Visible Band-Aids on others cause systemic uncertainty. Ear-inspections and cotton buds are OCD efforts to ensure "perfection."

Direct Protocol: Cotton Bud/Ear Boundary & Band-Aid Script

State firmly: "My ears are clean. Cotton buds are away." Put all cotton buds in locked or out-of-reach storage.

The Band-Aid Script: "Mom's arm is healing. The Band-Aid is working. We are finished talking about it." Diversion must immediately follow.

📱 Phone Wiping & Audio Control

Deleting notifications represents her attempt to keep her digital ecosystem perfectly neat. Restricting music/stereo play is a protective effort to reduce auditory chaos.

Direct Protocol: Let Her Delete & "My Turn" Visual

Let her delete: Allow deletion of harmless system assets. Use parental control locks to prevent deletion of critical communication tools or contacts.

The "My Turn" Visual: Use a laminated double-sided token for the stereo: "Side A: Parent's Turn / Side B: Her Turn". When Side A is active, she cannot modify input.

👤 "Mr. Minton" Relationship & PDA Boundary Plan

Her comment "By(e) Mr. Minton," paired with anxieties about "going back to school," confirms she perceives the support worker as a source of routine expectations and direct behaviour challenges.

1. Neutral, High-Structure Re-Entry

Do not over-explain or try to "win" a warm connection right away. Focus on highly predictable, low-demand activities she has complete control over. Use declarative language.

2. Establishing Unified Rules

If she attempts to hit you or force boundaries upon you, she must experience the same immediate physical distance protocol as parents. Demonstrate structured, predictable boundaries.

Crisis Management & Safety Plan
Bronwyn's crisis protocol and the Calm Blue Chair Rule
1. Immediate Communication
Remain Calm
Consistently remind Bronwyn "Everything will be ok."
Cantonese Language Cue
If she is speaking in Cantonese, remind her to be calm and breathe in/out slowly.
Limit Input
Do not offer any other instructions while she is in an escalated state.
2. Assess Escalation

Assess Bronwyn's ability to calm herself. Once she demonstrates she is returning to a calm state, she can be gently reminded of the safety rules.

3. The "Calm Blue Chair" Rule
🛒 At the Shop
Hitting is unacceptable and unsafe. She cannot remain at the shop. She must go to the calming blue chair in the park with a support person. Mum will call the support worker to assess safety before she can return.
🏠 At Home
Hitting is unacceptable and unsafe. She cannot remain at home. She must go to the calming blue chair in the park with a support person. Mum will call the support worker to assess safety before she can return.
🌳 In the Community
Hitting is unacceptable and unsafe. No activities. She must go to the calming blue chair in the park with a support person. Mum will call the support worker to assess safety before returning home or to the shop.
